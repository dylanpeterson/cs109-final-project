Background

Rice is the staple food for over 50% of the world's population. Therefore, providing methods for farmers to produce more rice and grow it faster would be very beneficial to feeding these populations. We wanted to look into statistical methods of predicting features that would provide better strains for these farmers. For example, faster maturity, disease resistance, and better crop output could have significant impacts on farm production as well as providing food to those who need it. In biology classes, Dylan and O'Neil have learned about Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms (SNPs), which are single base pair mutations within an organism's genome. These SNPs can have impacts on the function of specific genes within organisms, which may result in different phenotypic features (characteristics of the organism). We have seen studies that have used SNP data to predict phenotypic features. Our group decided that we wanted to use SNP data downloaded from Amazon Web Services, as well as phenotypic features scraped from The International Rice Genebank Collection Information System (IRGCIS), to determine if we can predict SNPs that are correlated with phenotypes that will be beneficial for rice farmers.




Exploratory Data Analysis







We decided to perform further analysis on the amount of time the rice strains take to grow to maturity. This phenotypic feature is important as it impacts how fast the rice can be harvested for food. Looking at the distribution of days to maturity in the data, it was fairly normally distributed. This is to be expected as it is a continuous phenotypic trait, combinatorial genetic factors coupled with environmental interactions produce normally looking distributions of phenotypes like height. (See the work of RA Fischer for more detail). We define ‘Fast’ growing strains as those that are in the shortest 15% of strains (approximately 107 days or less).




Feature Models





Modeling Utilizing SNP Data

















From there, we decided to begin utilizing the SNP data in our modeling. After it went through an time consuming cleaning process, the data was ready to be used to predict days to maturity. We ran 3 machine learning models on groups of 50,000 SNPs to see how they well each model predicted days to maturity. After picking the best model, we re-ran it on all of the 909,000 SNPs and found which ones it utilized the most in deciding if it was fast or slow. We selected 50,000 of these ‘top’ SNPs and ran trained another model on it, producing accuracy results of 96.3%.




Results







In the end, we combined this model with the previous model using phenotypic features and were able to further raise our prediction accuracy to 96.7%.

Takeaways: From our models, we see that the 50,000 most influential SNPs have a very high accuracy of predicting fast-maturing strains of rice with Linear Support Vector Classification (SVC). SVC also does a good job of predicting fast-maturing strains of rice with sets of 50,000 SNPs. However, Logistic Regression with Lasso and a Pipeline model, which runs Linear SVC and selects the top k features and perfoms another SVC on it, do not have much predicting power. They mainly predict all of the strains to not be fast growing, which is the same as a model that strictly predicts every strain to not be fast growing (accuracy of .85). This makes sense as both models were set to minimize features - LogLasso by design, and the pipeline model by our choice of selecting the k=25 best to perform the second SVC on. This means there were not likely many SNPs that could singularly impact the models, but rather in large number they tipped the scale to either fast or not fast predictions in the 50k features in one SVM run. From the models created from regression on source country and variety type, we were able to create a model with predictive power. When we combined the best SVC model (trained with the 50,000 most influential SNP) with the feature model by performing a logistic regression on the predicions made by both, we were able to increas the accuracy to 96.7%

From our analysis of correlation of SNPs with fast-maturing strains, we found that thousands of the SNPs are significantly correlated with fast-maturing strains. Although, this large number of SNPs was unexpected, it makes sense because maturing fast is most likely not a quality that is only influenced by just one gene; the interaction among many genes is likely more determinant of the speed of maturation. Therefore it makes sense that many SNPs occuring in various genes would have an influence on speed of maturation.
